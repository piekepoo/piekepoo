X, Y, Zephir



w/ Gabe Webb and David Cotarelo

Summary

X,Y, Zephir was a project hosted during the New Media and New Technologies Exhibition in july 2018. Its aim was to take the natural flow of the wind and juxtapose it with technology. How could we captivate patterns of the wind and translate it into other sensations? Wind patterns were recorded through balloons controlling XYZ sensors and accelerometers and mapped onto a board of light (LEDs) using an Arduino.

Concept

A gust from the sea propels a sailboat, sets the blades of a windmill turning, rustles a thicket of reeds or tussles our hair. We experience wind as a kinetic force, but can its sensations--soothing and severe--be translated into other sensory expressions?

Motivation

During the starting stage of the project we spent various meetings thinking of a project idea. In one of our brainstormings we were hit by a sudden flow of ideas which came from our observation of the many plants and trees that can be seen outside the room. We saw that all these plants were in constant movement and that this movement was driven by the forces of the wind. From this observation we started to come up with various way in which we could harness data from these forces of the wind and use it to reproduce similar patterns to the resulting movements of plants and trees. We eventually decided that the best way to harness this data would be to attach sensors to some moving objects and we decided that we could replicate the structure of the wind by using a matrix of balloons. With a finalised concept where a matrix of balloons can be affected by incoming winds, creating a pattern in the movement of these balloons, to then be reproduced by the control and sequencing of a matrix of LEDs.

Implementation

For this project, we used a great amount of hardware and physical computing. We placed 15 sensors, one attached per balloon that simultaneously gathered data from all of those sensors. We Arduino’s, accelerometers and gyroscopes to collect this data and translate it to something meaningful with coding. We translated these values from the accelerometers and gyroscopes to HSL values of RGB LEDs that would be distributed along a similar matrix to the balloon’s matrix. We used an Arduino to connect the sensors to the lights and to run the code and communicated between them with wires. We hang both the frame with balloons and the frame with the lights on the ceiling so that the direct effect of the wind on the balloon became visible to the audience.
